Investigative Report and Public Integrity Audit: Cameron-Beach Site Acquisitions and Associated Background Profiles
Property Ownership and Municipal Transaction Context
The public acquisition of the contiguous real estate parcels located at 17631 Cameron Lane and 17642 Beach Boulevard in Huntington Beach, California, represents a highly structured municipal transaction executed by the Huntington Beach Housing Authority and the City Council. Spanning approximately 1.6 acres, the combined site was acquired for a total capital expenditure of $6,094,847.
The primary funding mechanism for this dual-parcel transaction was the municipal Low and Moderate Income Housing Asset Fund (LMIHAF), which holds the restricted capital assets of the city’s former redevelopment agency. Under California state law, LMIHAF assets are strictly restricted to the creation, preservation, or improvement of housing for low- and moderate-income households. Municipal records indicate that the Huntington Beach Housing Authority actively initiated the acquisition to satisfy long-term affordable housing objectives and to prevent the mandatory reversion of excess LMIHAF capital to the State of California.
The structural mechanics of the acquisition reveal a deliberate tax-avoidance and asset-holding strategy executed by the sellers. Although the City Council authorized the acquisition of the Cameron Lane site and secured a purchase option for the Beach Boulevard site on February 17, 2020, the closing of the Beach Boulevard parcel was deferred to January 5, 2021, at the explicit request of the sellers for tax-planning purposes.
To facilitate the immediate municipal use of the Beach Boulevard site prior to the transfer of title, the City Manager executed a Ground Lease Agreement with Shigeru Yamada and Mitsuru Yamada, effective August 19, 2020. This ground lease authorized an interim lease rate of $15,000 per month, not to exceed a total of $120,000, funded through restricted COVID-19 Response Funds and the LMIHAF. CBRE represented both the municipality and the sellers’ family trusts in the negotiations.

	Municipal Operation and the Huntington Beach Navigation Center
Following the acquisition, the combined parcels were designated for a temporary municipal utility: the Huntington Beach Navigation Center (HBNC), an emergency homeless shelter. The rapid construction of the HBNC during the fall of 2020 was facilitated by a $4,206,214 infusion of restricted federal and state COVID-19 response funding, allowing the facility's design capacity to scale from an initial 75-bed shelter to a 174-bed congregate housing center.
This rapid capacity expansion carried significant regional law enforcement implications. Under federal court precedents governing municipal jurisdictions in Orange County, local authorities cannot enforce quality-of-life regulations or anti-camping ordinances unless they can demonstrate shelter bed capacity for at least 60% of their unsheltered homeless population. Establishing the 174-bed shelter satisfied this 60% threshold, granting the City of Huntington Beach immediate legal authority to enforce municipal anti-vagrancy ordinances.
The facility is operated under a municipal service contract by Mercy House Living Centers, Inc.. The operating structure of the HBNC enforces strict access controls: admission is limited to referrals with verified ties to Huntington Beach, and resident movement is tightly controlled via a dedicated shuttle service. To align capacity with actual utilization trends, the bed count was reduced to 164 in July 2025. During the 2024 calendar year, the facility maintained an average daily occupancy of 158 residents (peaking at 168), reflecting a 91% average occupancy rate, while serving 82 seniors and 18 veterans.

Litigation Profile and Environmental Allegations
The federal lawsuit (Case No. 8:2026cv00348, filed February 14, 2026, in the United States District Court for the Central District of California, assigned to Judge John W. Holcomb and referred to Magistrate Judge Autumn D. Spaeth) seeks recovery for environmental cleanup expenses and personal injury damages. The formal complaints outline a detailed history of contamination and regulatory failure:
1. Subsurface Contamination Profile: Both the Cameron and Beach sites carry long-term soil and groundwater contamination issues. The OCHCA certified the cleanup case as closed on August 21, 2020, on the condition that a permanent asphalt pavement cover be maintained to encapsulate the subsurface contaminants, which include hexavalent chromium (at 49 times the legal safety limit), lead, arsenic, and pesticide residues (Toxaphene, DDE).
2. Containment Degradation & Toxic Exposure: Knabb's federal filings allege that Mercy House systematically degraded this protective containment barrier through daily high-pressure power washing. This procedure allegedly mobilized and aerosolized toxic heavy metals and pesticides, directly exposing Knabb and other unhoused residents. This toxic exposure allegedly resulted in permanent physical injuries and systemic illness.
3. Allegations of Database Fraud: Knabb's notices to the court further allege that regulatory agencies engaged in systematic "address manipulation" and parcel identity fraud across national environmental databases to conceal an unabandoned industrial subsurface well dating to 1969, thereby bypassing statutory environmental reviews under the California Environmental Quality Act (CEQA).

	Regulatory Compliance and Fiduciary Standards in Property Transactions
A forensic analysis of the property transactions and the subsequent establishment of the municipal Navigation Center highlights several regulatory compliance frameworks. When a public housing authority acquires real estate with known toxic contamination, strict adherence to disclosure and safe harbor rules is required to shield the municipality and the operating non-profit from federal and state liability.
Fiduciary Disclosure and Safe Harbor Mandates
Under SEC Regulation S-K, Item 404(a), public entities are mandated to disclose "Related Person" transactions exceeding $120,000 in which an executive officer, director, or immediate family member has a material interest. Under Item 404(a), immediate family members include spouses, parents, stepparents, children, stepchildren, siblings, and in-laws.
Similarly, safe harbor provisions under Delaware General Corporation Law (DGCL) Section 144 require disinterested board or stockholder approval following full disclosure of all material facts to insulate conflicted transactions from rigorous "Entire Fairness" judicial reviews. For tax-exempt operators like Mercy House, the IRS utilizes Schedule L of Form 990 to monitor excess benefit transactions, loans, grants, and business dealings involving interested persons.
When fiduciaries conceal material conflicts or environmental risks from audit committees, it represents a structural breakdown of internal controls. Failing to provide external auditors with a complete mapping of related-party relationships violates PCAOB Standard 2410 and strips the board of independent oversight. Under the Sarbanes-Oxley Act (SOX) Section 906, officer certification of non-compliant filings carrying knowing or willful intent to defraud is subject to maximum federal penalties of up to $5,000,000 and 20 years of imprisonment.
